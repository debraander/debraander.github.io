Bodies

Most flies have a simple body made of one or two elements.

  • Common Elements
  • Floss
  • Spun Deer-Hair
  • Quill
  • Dubbed
  • Herl

Wingcase: The protrusions on top of many nymph patterns, representing undeveloped wings.

Abdomen: The long, usually thin, part of the fly that begins just behind the wings or wingcase.

Thorax: The shorter, thicker section near the front of the fly.

Tag: A few turns of contrasting-color thread, floss, or tinsel at or near the hook bend.

Butt: Several turns of material, such as herl, at the bend of the hook.

Head: Made with extra thread wraps, by leaving butt ends of wing fibers exposed and trimming them.

Available in both silks and synthetics, floss is used to form a fine, smooth body profile. It can also be used to form a base layer or underbody.

The hollow fibers of deer-body hair make it unbeatable for tying bass bugs. It floats well and can be trimmed to create a fat mouse or froglike appearance.

Quill bodies simulate the shiny, segmented look of a natural mayfly and are used on many dry- and wet-fly patterns.

The most common type of body material, dubbing can be used to produce a variety of body shapes and thicknesses for almost all types of flies.

Herl is used mainly for dry flies, wet flies, and nymphs, but many fly fisherman believe that it increases the effectiveness of any fly.
