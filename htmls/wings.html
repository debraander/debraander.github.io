Wings

Wings imitate the profile of a natural insect. They also slow the dry fly's descent so it settles gently to the surface. They add color and action to wet flies and streamers.

  • Hackle Point
  • Hair
  • Quill
  • Parachute

Hackle-point wings are made from the tips, or points, of a hackle feather. They produce a realistic wing profile, but add very little weight to the finished fly.

Hair wings are usually made of deer, calf, or elk hair, or a synthetic substitute. These wings are durable, buoyant, and are easy to see on the surface of the water.

Tent-style wings are made with a folded quill segment, tied in over the back to mimic the folded wings of an adult insect.

Used only on surface patterns, the parachute-style wing forms the base around which the hackle is wrapped horizontally. This wing-and-hackle combination causes the fly to settle slowly to the water, like a parachute.
